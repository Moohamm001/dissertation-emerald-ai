# 4.1 The Evolution of Credit Scoring: From Discriminant Analysis to Statistical Learning

## Thesis (one sentence)

Credit scoring has moved through three eras — ratio-based discriminant analysis (1960s–1980s), regulator-default logistic regression (1990s–2010s), and ensemble/ML benchmarking (2010s onward) — and Dumitrescu et al. (2022) recently reframed the long-running accuracy/interpretability trade-off as a design choice rather than a fixed frontier.

## Argumentative arc

1. The lineage runs Altman 1968 → discriminant analysis → logistic regression as the regulatory default for four decades, valued for monotonicity, sparsity, and direct mapping to odds and points-based scorecards [[hand1997statistical]], [[thomas2000survey]].
2. Migration to non-parametric learning began with NN and SVMs in the late 1990s but stalled on opacity grounds — incompatible with right-to-explanation regimes embedded in ECOA, GDPR Article 22, and Basel risk-weight reporting [[baesens2003benchmark]].
3. Lessmann et al. (2015) extended Baesens to 41 classifiers and established two enduring findings: ensemble methods dominate single learners, and the marginal accuracy gains from architectural sophistication shrink as dataset size grows [[lessmann2015benchmark]].
4. Dumitrescu et al. (2022) shows penalised LR + explicit tree-derived interaction terms can close most of the gap to XGBoost — so accuracy and interpretability are not on a frontier, they are a design choice [[dumitrescu2022ml]].

## Key sources

## Counter-evidence to keep honest

- Industry practice still defaults to logistic regression in regulated bureaux despite 25 years of ML evidence — adoption lag is not just opacity, it is also vendor lock-in and validation cost. Worth a sentence acknowledging this.
- Lessmann's "ensembles dominate" finding may not generalise to the data-scarce tabular regime more typical of green lending where dataset sizes < 50k rows.

## Open holes

- Need a primary citation for the claim that "logistic regression has remained the regulatory default for over four decades" — currently asserted on the strength of Hand/Henley/Thomas.
- The "right-to-explanation" reading of GDPR Article 22 is contested in legal literature (Wachter et al. vs Selbst & Powles 2017). Could cite Selbst & Powles for nuance.

## Drafted prose (currently in proposal_second_draft.docx)

> Modern credit scoring traces its lineage to Altman's Z-score [Altman, 1968] and Beaver's univariate ratio analyses [Beaver, 1966], later formalised through linear discriminant analysis and, eventually, logistic regression — which has remained the regulatory default for over four decades owing to its monotonicity, sparsity, and direct mapping to odds and points-based scorecards [Hand and Henley, 1997; Thomas, 2000]. The migration to non-parametric learning began with neural networks and SVMs in the late 1990s, but adoption stalled on opacity grounds: regulators viewed black-box models as incompatible with the right-to-explanation regimes embedded in ECOA, GDPR Article 22, and Basel risk-weight reporting. Baesens et al. (2003) carried out one of the first systematic comparative evaluations of classification algorithms on real credit datasets, demonstrating that neural networks and SVMs consistently outperformed logistic regression. Lessmann et al. (2015) extended this benchmark to 41 classifiers across eight credit datasets and established two findings that still shape the field: heterogeneous ensemble methods (random forest, gradient boosting, stacking) dominate single learners, and the marginal accuracy gains from architectural sophistication shrink as datasets grow. Dumitrescu et al. (2022) recently demonstrated that interpretable logistic regression augmented with explicit tree-derived interaction terms can close most of the gap to XGBoost — reframing the explainability/accuracy trade-off as a design choice rather than a fixed frontier.
