# 4.2 Gradient-Boosted Decision Trees as the Tabular State of the Art

## Thesis (one sentence)

For heterogeneous tabular data at 10³–10⁶ rows — exactly EMERALD-AI's regime — gradient-boosted decision trees are the consistent empirical winner, offer native monotonic-constraint support and polynomial-time TreeSHAP attributions, and remain regulator-friendly for credit deployment.

## Argumentative arc

1. The three modern GBDT implementations — XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost — solve different parts of the same problem: scaling (LightGBM), categorical handling without leakage (CatBoost), regularisation + sparsity-aware splits (XGBoost) [[chen2016xgboost]], [[ke2017lightgbm]], [[prokhorenkova2018catboost]].
2. Independent benchmarks consistently rank GBDTs at parity with — and often above — deep tabular architectures on moderate-scale classification [[shwartz2022deep]], [[grinsztajn2022trees]].
3. Two properties make GBDTs uniquely suited to credit risk: (a) monotonic constraints let modellers enforce directionality (higher credit score must not decrease creditworthiness) that regulators demand; (b) TreeSHAP yields exact polynomial-time Shapley attributions, making per-decision explanations production-feasible [[lundberg2020trees]].

## Counter-evidence to keep honest

- Some recent industry results (notably from large tech firms with very large tabular datasets) suggest deep tabular models can win when n > 10⁶, especially with self-supervised pre-training. Acknowledge this; EMERALD-AI's n ≈ 14k is firmly in GBDT territory.
- GBDTs do not extrapolate beyond the training distribution; for green loan modelling where macro regimes change, this is a deployment-time concern.

## Open holes

- The claim that "regulators increasingly demand monotonicity" is asserted — could be strengthened with a citation to specific supervisory guidance (e.g., Fed SR 11-7, EBA GL/2017/16).
- No explicit comparison of CatBoost's target-encoder vs leave-one-out target encoding for our specific data — worth noting as an empirical question rather than literature claim.

## Drafted prose (currently in proposal_second_draft.docx)

> For heterogeneous tabular data with mixed-type features, missing values, and moderate scale (10³–10⁶ rows), gradient-boosted decision trees (GBDT) have been the consistent empirical winner. XGBoost [Chen and Guestrin, 2016] introduced second-order Newton boosting with L1/L2 regularisation and sparsity-aware split finding; LightGBM [Ke et al., 2017] added histogram-based binning and leaf-wise growth for order-of-magnitude training speedups; CatBoost [Prokhorenkova et al., 2018] introduced ordered boosting and target-statistics encoding that eliminate the prediction-shift bias arising from naive target encoding. Independent benchmarks consistently rank GBDTs at parity with — and often above — deep tabular architectures on classification problems of the scale considered here [Shwartz-Ziv and Armon, 2022; Grinsztajn et al., 2022]. Two properties make GBDTs particularly well-suited to credit risk: (i) native support for monotonic constraints, which let modellers enforce the directionality of features (e.g., higher credit score must not decrease creditworthiness probability) that regulators increasingly demand; and (ii) integration with TreeSHAP [Lundberg et al., 2020], which yields exact Shapley attributions in polynomial time — making per-decision explanations cheap enough to ship in production.
