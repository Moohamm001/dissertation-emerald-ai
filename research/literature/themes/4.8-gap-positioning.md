# 4.8 Identified Research Gap and the Positioning of EMERALD-AI

## Thesis (one sentence)

There is no published, empirically validated, regulator-ready ML credit-scoring framework trained on real green-loan transaction data that simultaneously delivers modern tabular benchmarking, calibration + uncertainty, multi-method explainability with fidelity validation, fairness/robustness audit, and a deployable interface — EMERALD-AI is positioned to occupy this gap end-to-end.

## The gap, decomposed

The gap is precise — it is the *intersection* of five requirements, each of which has independent literature but none of which has been combined on real green-loan data:

1. **Modern tabular learners benchmarked against interpretable baselines under identical preprocessing.** Conventional credit literature benchmarks ML methods [[lessmann2015benchmark]], [[dumitrescu2022ml]], but never on green loans.
2. **Post-hoc calibration and distribution-free uncertainty quantification.** General ML calibration literature is mature [[platt1999probabilistic]], [[zadrozny2002isotonic]], [[angelopoulos2023conformal]], but largely absent from green-credit work.
3. **Multi-method explainability stack with empirical fidelity validation.** SHAP/LIME/DiCE are mature individually [[lundberg2017shap]], [[ribeiro2016lime]], [[mothilal2020dice]]; their critical limitations are documented [[rudin2019stop]], [[slack2020fooling]]; Quantus enables fidelity validation [[hedstrom2023quantus]] — but combined into a credit-grade audit stack on green data, no.
4. **Fairness audit appropriate to green lending's industry/geography proxies.** Fairness toolkits exist [[bellamy2018aif360]]; impossibility results are well-known [[chouldechova2017fair]]; but no published green-credit work applies AIF360-style audits on green-loan-relevant proxy axes.
5. **Deployable interface that lets lending officers act on outputs.** This is the production gap — model code released on GitHub is common, full-stack decision-support is rare.

## Why this gap matters now

- The EU AI Act [[euAiAct2021]] enters force in stages from 2025–2027; credit-scoring AI providers will face Annex III obligations including explainability, robustness, and post-market monitoring. Frameworks designed around these obligations have first-mover regulatory advantage.
- Green loan origination is growing at >40% CAGR [[lma2023glp]]; the cost of mis-allocation scales with portfolio size, so the marginal value of a green-specific framework grows over time.
- ESG-rating disagreement [[berg2022esg]] is being used by US conservative state attorneys-general to challenge ESG-aware investment decisions; a credit-scoring framework that does *not* lean on third-party ESG scores has resilience against this political backdrop.

## What EMERALD-AI contributes

Crisply, the dissertation contributes the *first* artefact that is simultaneously:
- empirically validated on real green-loan data,
- benchmarked across modern tabular learners (LR, SVM, RF, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, MLP, FT-Transformer),
- post-hoc calibrated and conformalised,
- explainable via a multi-method stack with fidelity validation,
- fairness- and robustness-audited on green-lending-appropriate proxy axes,
- deployable as a full-stack MLOps-grade application.

## Open holes

- The "first" claim is strong. To defend it, the supervisor will reasonably ask: have we systematically searched for prior work? A Scopus/Web of Science search with the strings `("green loan" OR "green credit") AND ("machine learning" OR "explainable")` is documented in [[gaps]]. Anything found that overlaps must be cited and differentiated, not ignored.
- We claim regulator-readiness; we do not yet have legal-counsel sign-off. The dissertation should describe the framework as "designed against EU AI Act obligations" not "compliant with".
