# 4.5 Green Finance, ESG Risk, and the Specificity of Sustainable Lending

## Thesis (one sentence)

Sustainable lending differs from conventional credit along three structural dimensions — back-loaded policy-sensitive cash flows, reputational/greenwashing tails, and jurisdictionally heterogeneous "green" classification — and no published ML credit-scoring framework engages with all three on real green-loan transaction data.

## Argumentative arc

1. **Cash-flow profile.** Green-purposed assets (solar PV, retrofits, EV fleets) have back-loaded, policy-sensitive cash flows. Linear repayment history under-models long-horizon regulatory and technology risk [[flammer2021corporate]], [[tang2020shareholders]].
2. **Reputational tail.** Greenwashing exposure can crystallise into both regulatory penalty (EU Taxonomy disclosure, SFDR) and franchise damage that materially affects loss-given-default outside the borrower's own balance sheet [[ehlers2017greenbond]].
3. **Definition heterogeneity.** What counts as "green" varies across jurisdictions — EU Taxonomy [[euTaxonomy2020]], Climate Bonds Standard [[cbi2023standard]], and emerging national taxonomies define eligibility differently. This introduces label noise into any portfolio assembled across regulatory regimes.
4. **ESG-rating unreliability.** Third-party ESG scores exhibit substantial inter-rater disagreement [[berg2022esg]], making them unreliable as direct model features. EMERALD-AI consequently does not use ESG scores as inputs.
5. **Climate-credit channel is now empirically established.** Recent work shows that climate-risk disclosure moves credit-default-swap term structure [[julianfklbel2022ask]], and that climate change and clean-technology capex enter EU credit-risk cycles measurably [[nawazishmirza2024effects]] — so the green-loan-versus-conventional-loan distinction is no longer just a sustainability narrative, it is a quantifiable risk channel. Macroprudential proposals to differentiate capital requirements on green vs. brown exposures [[yannisdafermos2021how]] underline that regulators may soon require lenders to model this channel explicitly.
6. **Gap.** No published work has built a credit-scoring framework that engages with all three structural specifics on real green-loan transaction data — this is the central justification for the dissertation. See [[themes/4.8-gap-positioning]].

## Counter-evidence to keep honest

- The bulk of "green finance" empirical literature studies green bonds, not green loans — extrapolation should be flagged. Worth saying explicitly: "Most evidence is from green bonds; loans are under-studied, which is itself part of the gap."
- The greenwashing tail risk is asserted as material; quantitative loss data on greenwashing-related credit events is thin. Acknowledge as a directional claim.

## Open holes

- No primary citation for the "global green loan market 40%+ CAGR since 2018" claim used in the intro. The LMA report likely contains it; need to verify and pin exact figure.
- The dataset's "green loan" labelling provenance is unclear — which taxonomy was used by the originating institution? Worth surfacing in the methodology's data section as a known limitation.
- No literature on "transition finance" vs "pure green" — could deserve a paragraph if the supervisor pushes on contemporary green finance debates.

## Drafted prose (currently in proposal_second_draft.docx)

> Sustainable lending differs from conventional consumer or commercial credit along three structural dimensions that current scorecards under-model. First, the cash-flow profile of green-purposed assets — solar PV, building retrofits, EV fleets — is back-loaded and policy-sensitive, exposing lenders to long-horizon regulatory and technology risk that linear repayment history does not capture [Flammer, 2021; Tang and Zhang, 2020]. Second, green loans carry a non-financial reputational tail: greenwashing exposure can crystallise into both regulatory penalty (EU Taxonomy disclosure, SFDR) and franchise damage that materially affects loss-given-default outside the borrower's own balance sheet [Ehlers and Packer, 2017]. Third, what counts as "green" is heterogeneous across jurisdictions and evolving — the EU Taxonomy [European Commission, 2020], the Climate Bonds Standard [CBI, 2023], and emerging national taxonomies each define eligibility differently, introducing label noise into any portfolio assembled across regulatory regimes. ESG scores themselves exhibit substantial inter-rater disagreement [Berg et al., 2022], making them unreliable as direct features. To date no published work has built a credit-scoring framework that engages with all three structural specifics on real green-loan transaction data.
