# 4.4 Class Imbalance, Calibration, and Uncertainty in Credit Risk

## Thesis (one sentence)

Credit datasets are intrinsically imbalanced (2–15% positive prevalence) and well-discriminating models are routinely mis-calibrated — both must be handled by leakage-safe in-fold resampling and post-hoc calibration, with split-conformal prediction supplying distribution-free uncertainty intervals; this triple-stack is largely absent from existing green-credit literature.

## Argumentative arc

1. Imbalance: naive training minimises overall error but underperforms the minority class. Interventions span resampling (SMOTE [[chawla2002smote]], ADASYN [[he2008adasyn]], SMOTE-NC for mixed types), cost-sensitive loss weighting, and focal loss [[lin2017focal]].
2. Resampling must occur strictly inside CV folds — a methodological error widespread in published credit-scoring work [[santos2018cvimbalanced]]. EMERALD-AI treats this as a hard rule.
3. Calibration: a model can rank applicants correctly (high AUC) while producing systematically biased probability estimates. This breaks expected-loss pricing, capital provisioning, and IRB-grade PD reporting. Post-hoc fixes: Platt scaling [[platt1999probabilistic]], isotonic regression [[zadrozny2002isotonic]], temperature scaling.
4. Uncertainty: split-conformal prediction [[vovk2005alrw]], [[angelopoulos2023conformal]] gives distribution-free per-applicant prediction intervals with guaranteed marginal coverage. The FCA and EBA increasingly cite this as best practice for high-risk AI.

## Counter-evidence to keep honest

- SMOTE has been criticised for generating implausible synthetic minority points in high-dimensional feature spaces. For tabular finance data with strong feature interactions, this is a real risk — worth running SMOTE-NC vs class-weighting head-to-head.
- Calibration via Platt scaling can degrade discrimination when applied to already-well-calibrated models. The choice of calibrator should itself be empirical, not default.
- Conformal prediction guarantees marginal coverage but not conditional coverage — a high-risk applicant may receive an overconfident interval. Mondrian conformal or adaptive variants would address this.

## Drafted prose (currently in proposal_second_draft.docx)

> Credit-default datasets are intrinsically imbalanced — typical positive (default) prevalence in well-underwritten portfolios sits in the 2–15% range. Naive training on imbalanced data produces classifiers that minimise overall error but underperform on the minority class that drives loss. Standard interventions include resampling (SMOTE [Chawla et al., 2002], SMOTE-NC for mixed types, ADASYN [He et al., 2008]), cost-sensitive loss reweighting, and focal loss [Lin et al., 2017]. Critically, resampling must occur strictly inside cross-validation folds to prevent test-set contamination — a methodological mistake that is widespread in published credit-scoring work [Santos et al., 2018]. A separate and often-overlooked concern is calibration: a model can rank applicants correctly (high AUC) while producing systematically biased probability estimates, which breaks expected-loss pricing, capital provisioning, and regulatory probability-of-default reporting under the Internal Ratings-Based approach. Platt scaling [Platt, 1999], isotonic regression [Zadrozny and Elkan, 2002], temperature scaling, and conformal prediction [Vovk et al., 2005; Angelopoulos and Bates, 2023] provide post-hoc calibration and distribution-free uncertainty quantification respectively, and are largely absent from existing green-credit literature. EMERALD-AI treats both as mandatory pipeline stages.
