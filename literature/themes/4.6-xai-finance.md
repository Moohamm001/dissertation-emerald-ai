# 4.6 Explainable AI in High-Stakes Finance

## Thesis (one sentence)

Explainability in ML credit scoring is not a single technique but a stack of complementary tools (global attribution, local attribution, counterfactual recourse) — and a growing critical literature shows post-hoc explanations of opaque models can be unreliable or even manipulable, motivating EMERALD-AI's hybrid approach: a constrained tree ensemble (inherently inspectable) layered with a multi-method explanation stack and empirical fidelity validation.

## Argumentative arc

1. **Three explanation layers, three questions.** Global attribution (mean |SHAP|, permutation importance) → "what does the model rely on overall?" Local attribution (TreeSHAP, LIME) → "why did this borrower receive this score?" Counterfactual (DiCE, Wachter-style) → "what would the borrower need to change to be approved?" — the form regulators increasingly favour because it is actionable [[mothilal2020dice]], [[wachter2017counterfactual]].
2. **SHAP's appeal is theoretical.** Lundberg & Lee proved SHAP is the unique attribution method satisfying local accuracy, missingness, and consistency [[lundberg2017shap]]. TreeSHAP extends this to exact polynomial-time attributions for tree ensembles [[lundberg2020trees]].
3. **But critical perspectives are growing.** Feature attribution can be misleading under feature correlation [[aas2021explaining]], [[janzing2020feature]]; counterfactuals can be unstable [[rawal2020beyond]]; post-hoc explanations of opaque models can be deliberately manipulated to hide bias [[slack2020fooling]].
4. **Rudin's dissent.** For high-stakes decisions, Rudin argues, inherently interpretable models should be preferred over post-hoc explanations of opaque ones [[rudin2019stop]]. This is a position the dissertation must engage with, not dismiss.
5. **EMERALD-AI's pragmatic middle path.** Deploy a constrained tree ensemble whose structure is itself inspectable; layer a multi-method explanation stack (TreeSHAP + KernelSHAP cross-check + counterfactuals + monotonic-constraint verification) rather than relying on a single attribution mechanism; empirically validate explanation faithfulness via Quantus [[hedstrom2023quantus]].

## Counter-evidence to keep honest

- The "Rudin position" if taken to its logical conclusion would push us toward something like an Explainable Boosting Machine (EBM, GA²M family) rather than XGBoost + SHAP. EMERALD-AI's pragmatic middle path is a defensible choice but not the only one — could acknowledge EBMs as an alternative we considered.
- Quantus's faithfulness metrics are themselves contested; reporting them is rigorous but not definitive.

## Open holes

- We do not yet engage with the LIME-vs-SHAP empirical comparison literature. Could add a short paragraph citing Camburu et al. or Doshi-Velez & Kim.
- The legal vs technical definitions of "explanation" diverge (Selbst & Powles, Edwards & Veale on right to explanation). Worth a footnote.
- Explainable Boosting Machine (EBM, InterpretML) is missing — a glaring omission given Rudin's affiliation. Stub: [[nori2019interpretml]] could be added.

## Drafted prose (currently in proposal_second_draft.docx)

> Explainability in ML credit scoring is not a single technique but a stack of complementary tools, each addressing a different audit question. Global feature attribution (permutation importance, mean |SHAP|) answers 'what does the model rely on at the portfolio level?'; local attribution (LIME [Ribeiro et al., 2016], TreeSHAP) answers 'why did this borrower receive this score?'; counterfactual explanations (DiCE [Mothilal et al., 2020], Wachter-style counterfactuals [Wachter et al., 2017]) answer 'what would the borrower need to change to be approved?' — the form regulators and consumer-advocacy bodies increasingly favour because it is actionable. SHAP's appeal derives from its grounding in cooperative game theory: it is the unique attribution method satisfying local accuracy, missingness, and consistency [Lundberg and Lee, 2017]. Critical perspectives, however, are growing. Feature attribution can be misleading under feature correlation [Aas et al., 2021; Janzing et al., 2020]; counterfactuals can be unstable [Rawal and Lakkaraju, 2020]; and post-hoc explanations of opaque models can be deliberately manipulated to hide bias [Slack et al., 2020]. Rudin (2019) goes further and argues that for high-stakes decisions, inherently interpretable models (constrained additive models, sparse decision lists) should be preferred over post-hoc explanations of opaque ones. EMERALD-AI takes the pragmatic middle path: it deploys a constrained tree ensemble whose structure is itself inspectable, and layers a multi-method explanation stack (TreeSHAP + KernelSHAP cross-check + counterfactuals + monotonic-constraint verification) rather than relying on a single attribution mechanism. Explanation faithfulness is itself empirically validated via the Quantus toolkit [Hedström et al., 2023].
