# 4.3 Tabular Deep Learning: Promise, Pitfalls, and Recent Advances

## Thesis (one sentence)

Modern tabular DL architectures (TabNet, FT-Transformer, SAINT, NODE) have not yet displaced well-tuned GBDTs on moderate-scale benchmarks; they earn their keep only in specific regimes (very large data, high-cardinality categoricals, multi-task pre-training) — none of which strictly hold for EMERALD-AI, so DL is included as an honest comparator rather than the headline approach.

## Argumentative arc

1. The family is rapidly evolving: TabNet introduced sequential attention with sparse feature masks doubling as feature attribution [[arik2021tabnet]]; FT-Transformer adapts the Transformer encoder via feature tokenisation [[gorishniy2021revisiting]]; SAINT combines row + column attention with contrastive pre-training [[somepalli2021saint]]; NODE built differentiable oblivious decision-tree ensembles [[popov2020node]].
2. The empirical picture is sober: on most public benchmarks of moderate size these architectures fail to beat well-tuned GBDTs [[borisov2022dnntabular]], [[grinsztajn2022trees]], [[shwartz2022deep]].
3. Deep tabular models earn their keep in three regimes — (a) very large datasets, (b) high-cardinality categoricals where learned embeddings outperform target encoding, (c) multi-task / self-supervised pre-training. For green credit scoring at n ≈ 14k, only (b) partially applies (Industry has ~30 levels, Borrower State ~50).
4. EMERALD-AI therefore includes a tuned FT-Transformer and an MLP as honest comparators, resisting the temptation to over-claim DL's necessity.

## Counter-evidence to keep honest

- Gorishniy et al.'s own benchmark shows FT-Transformer winning on several datasets — the "GBDTs always win" narrative is too strong.
- Self-supervised pre-training (SAINT, SubTab) could in principle exploit unlabelled green-finance data; if a larger unlabelled corpus were obtainable, the case for DL would strengthen.

## Open holes

- We do not yet have a dataset-scale-vs-DL-advantage curve to cite. Worth noting as an empirical question the dissertation can speak to.
- No mention of recent transformer-based foundation models for tabular data (e.g. TabPFN, TabuLa-8B) — these may deserve a paragraph if the supervisor pushes on "frontier" methods. Currently a stub: [[hollmann2023tabpfn]] would be the citation.

## Drafted prose (currently in proposal_second_draft.docx)

> The push to extend deep learning's vision and language successes to tabular data has produced a rapidly evolving family of architectures. TabNet [Arik and Pfister, 2021] introduced sequential attention with sparse feature masks that double as built-in feature attribution; FT-Transformer [Gorishniy et al., 2021] adapted the Transformer encoder to tabular features via feature-tokenisation; SAINT [Somepalli et al., 2021] combined row- and column-attention with contrastive pre-training; NODE [Popov et al., 2020] built differentiable oblivious decision-tree ensembles trainable end-to-end. The empirical picture, however, is more sober: on most public benchmarks of moderate size these architectures fail to beat well-tuned GBDTs [Borisov et al., 2022; Grinsztajn et al., 2022]. Deep tabular models earn their keep in specific regimes — very large datasets, the presence of high-cardinality categoricals where learned embeddings outperform target encoding, and multi-task or self-supervised pre-training scenarios where representation transfer matters. For green credit scoring at the scale of approximately 14,000 transactions, this dissertation includes a tuned FT-Transformer and MLP as honest comparators while resisting the temptation to over-claim deep learning's necessity.
